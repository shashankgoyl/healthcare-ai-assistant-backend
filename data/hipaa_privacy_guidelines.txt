=== HIPAA PRIVACY AND SECURITY OVERVIEW ===

WHAT IS HIPAA?
The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 is a federal law that sets national standards for protecting sensitive patient health information. HIPAA includes the Privacy Rule, the Security Rule, and the Breach Notification Rule.

PROTECTED HEALTH INFORMATION (PHI):
PHI includes any individually identifiable health information that relates to:
- Past, present, or future physical or mental health conditions
- Provision of healthcare to an individual
- Past, present, or future payment for healthcare

PHI identifiers include:
- Name, address, dates (birth, admission, discharge)
- Phone numbers, fax numbers, email addresses
- Social Security numbers, medical record numbers
- Health plan beneficiary numbers, account numbers
- Certificate/license numbers, VINs, device identifiers
- Web URLs, IP addresses, biometric identifiers
- Full-face photographs, any other unique identifying number

PATIENT RIGHTS UNDER HIPAA:
1. Right to Access: Patients have the right to inspect and receive copies of their health records. Requests must be fulfilled within 30 days (one 30-day extension permitted).

2. Right to Amend: Patients may request corrections to their health records if they believe information is inaccurate or incomplete.

3. Right to Accounting of Disclosures: Patients can request a list of who has received their PHI for purposes other than treatment, payment, or healthcare operations.

4. Right to Restrict: Patients may request restrictions on how their information is used or disclosed, though covered entities are not always required to agree.

5. Right to Confidential Communications: Patients can request that communications occur through alternative means or at alternative locations.

6. Right to Notice: Patients have the right to receive the facility's Notice of Privacy Practices.

7. Right to File a Complaint: Patients may file complaints with the facility or with the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS).

PERMITTED USES AND DISCLOSURES:
PHI may be used or disclosed without patient authorization for:
- Treatment purposes (sharing between treating providers)
- Payment (billing and claims processing)
- Healthcare operations (quality improvement, training, audits)
- Public health activities (disease reporting, FDA reporting)
- Law enforcement as required by law
- Emergency situations
- Judicial proceedings (court orders, subpoenas)
- Research with proper protocols and IRB approval

REQUIRED AUTHORIZATIONS:
Written patient authorization is required for:
- Marketing purposes
- Sale of PHI
- Most research uses
- Psychotherapy notes
- Substance abuse treatment records (42 CFR Part 2)

MINIMUM NECESSARY STANDARD:
When using or disclosing PHI, only the minimum necessary information should be shared to accomplish the intended purpose. This applies to routine disclosures but not to treatment disclosures between providers.

SECURITY RULE OVERVIEW:
The Security Rule applies specifically to electronic PHI (ePHI) and requires:

Administrative Safeguards:
- Security management process including risk analysis
- Assigned security responsibility
- Workforce training and access management
- Evaluation of security measures

Physical Safeguards:
- Facility access controls
- Workstation use policies
- Device and media controls

Technical Safeguards:
- Access controls (unique user IDs, emergency access procedures)
- Audit controls
- Integrity controls
- Transmission security (encryption)

BREACH NOTIFICATION:
A breach is an impermissible use or disclosure of unsecured PHI. If a breach occurs:
- Affected individuals must be notified within 60 days
- The Secretary of HHS must be notified
- Breaches affecting 500+ individuals require media notification in the affected state
- A breach risk assessment should be conducted to determine if notification is required

BUSINESS ASSOCIATE AGREEMENTS (BAAs):
Any vendor, contractor, or business partner who handles PHI on behalf of a covered entity must sign a Business Associate Agreement. This agreement outlines the permitted uses, required safeguards, and liability.

PENALTIES FOR VIOLATIONS:
- Tier 1 (unknowing): $100–$50,000 per violation
- Tier 2 (reasonable cause): $1,000–$50,000 per violation
- Tier 3 (willful neglect, corrected): $10,000–$50,000 per violation
- Tier 4 (willful neglect, not corrected): $50,000+ per violation
- Criminal penalties may also apply for intentional misuse

PATIENT RESPONSIBILITIES:
- Provide accurate information to support your care
- Inform us of any changes to your contact information
- Notify us if you wish to limit who can access your information
- Review your Notice of Privacy Practices

STAFF RESPONSIBILITIES:
- Complete annual HIPAA training
- Access only PHI necessary for job duties
- Report suspected breaches immediately
- Never share login credentials
- Secure devices and lock screens when not in use
- Do not discuss patient information in public areas

FOR QUESTIONS OR COMPLAINTS:
Contact our Privacy Officer or submit concerns through the patient portal. You may also contact the HHS Office for Civil Rights.
